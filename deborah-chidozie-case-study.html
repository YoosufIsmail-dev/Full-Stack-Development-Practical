Overview

The Challenge

Millions of people in Nigeria and across West Africa remain unbanked or underbanked — not due to lack of desire, but because existing digital banking products are designed for a different user. Complex onboarding flows, financial jargon, and interfaces that assume literacy levels that many users don't have create invisible walls to inclusion.

I was brought in as the lead product designer to rethink the core user journey — from account creation through daily money management — with empathy at the center of every decision.

Problem Space

What was broken

01
Onboarding Drop-off
78% of new users abandoned sign-up before completing identity verification. The flow required 11 screens with dense form fields and no progress indication.
02
Language Barrier
Financial terminology was used without explanation. Terms like "beneficiary", "standing order", and "NUBAN" were incomprehensible to first-time users.
03
Low Trust Signals
Users expressed deep distrust of digital money — a barrier rooted in past fraud experiences and a lack of visible security reassurance in the interface.
04
Poor Error Recovery
When transactions failed, error messages were cryptic codes rather than actionable guidance. Users had no clear path forward and often abandoned the task entirely.
Research

What we learned from real people

We conducted 24 in-depth interviews and 6 contextual observation sessions across Lagos and Ibadan, supplemented by a diary study with 40 participants over 3 weeks.

/ 01
Trust is earned through social proof, not institutions. Users trusted recommendations from family members over bank branding. Peer testimonials were more persuasive than security certifications.
/ 02
Voice and visual over text. 38% of participants preferred voice-guided flows or icon-only interfaces. Many were semi-literate or used phones in low-light conditions.
/ 03
Fear of irreversible actions. The single biggest concern was sending money to the wrong person. Users wanted multiple confirmation steps — even if it made transactions slower.
/ 04
Context interruptions are constant. Most transactions were attempted during multitasking — on buses, in markets, between calls. Flows needed to survive a 5-minute interruption gracefully.
/ 05
Data costs create real friction. Heavy animations and large media assets caused noticeable load times on 3G. Users on prepaid data were acutely aware of every page load.
Design Process

How we got to the solution

01 —
Discover
User interviews, competitive audit, and heuristic evaluation of the existing product.
02 —
Define
Affinity mapping, persona synthesis, and HMW statements to align the team on the problem.
03 —
Ideate
Design sprints, crazy-8s, and concept sketching with the engineering team to explore the solution space.
04 —
Prototype
Low-fi wireframes → mid-fi flows → interactive high-fi Figma prototypes across 3 iteration rounds.
05 —
Test
Moderated usability testing with 18 participants, Maze for unmoderated flows, and A/B testing post-launch.

Reflections

What I'd carry forward

◈
Design for the edges, benefit the center
When you design for users with low literacy, intermittent connectivity, and distrust of institutions — you end up with a product that's better for every user. Accessibility is a quality multiplier.
◈
Context is not a constraint — it's a creative brief
Understanding that users transact on moving buses or in noisy markets didn't limit the design — it made it sharper. Real environmental constraints produce more honest solutions.
◈
Trust is designed, not assumed
Every screen is an opportunity to signal safety. Receipts, confirmations, and even error messages are moments to reinforce that the user's money is protected. We treated trust as a feature, not a legal disclaimer.
◈
Involve engineers in the problem, not just the solution
Our most creative solutions came from early engineering collaboration during ideation. Performance constraints surfaced in week 1 — not in QA — made the final product measurably faster.
